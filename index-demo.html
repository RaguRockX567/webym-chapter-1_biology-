DIVERSITY IN THE LIVING WORLD

Biology is the science that studies life forms and the processes that keep them alive. The living world shows a great variety of living organisms. Early humans could easily see the difference between things that were not alive and things that were alive. They worshipped some of the non-living things like wind, sea, and fire, and also some animals and plants. A common feature of both living and non-living things was that they created feelings of awe or fear. The detailed description of living organisms, including human beings, started much later in history. Societies that looked at biology only from a human-centered view could make only limited progress in biological knowledge. Later, systematic and large-scale descriptions of life forms required proper systems of identification, naming (nomenclature), and classification.

The most important result of these studies was the discovery that living organisms share similarities with each other, both among those living at the same time (horizontally) and between present-day organisms and those that lived in the past (vertically). The realization that all living organisms today are related to each other and also to all organisms that ever lived on Earth was a great revelation. It made humans feel humble and encouraged cultural movements to conserve biodiversity. In the following chapters of this unit, there will be a description and classification of animals and plants from the perspective of a taxonomist.

  • Won the Crafoord Prize (1999)

THE LIVING WORLD

  • The living world is wonderful and full of amazing variety, with organisms found in extraordinary habitats like cold mountains, forests, oceans, lakes, deserts, and hot springs.
  • The beauty of living beings, such as a galloping horse, migrating birds, valleys of flowers, or even an attacking shark, creates awe and wonder.
  • Ecological conflict and cooperation among individuals in a population, among populations in a community, and even the molecular activities inside a cell make us think deeply about what life really is.
  • The question "What is life?" has two parts: one technical, asking what makes something living compared to non-living, and one philosophical, asking about the purpose of life.

DIVERSITY IN THE LIVING WORLD

  • Around us, there is a huge variety of living organisms — plants, animals, insects, birds, pets, and even tiny organisms we cannot see with our eyes.
  • The more area we observe, like a dense forest, the more species we find.
  • About 1.7–1.8 million species are known, and new ones are still being discovered.
  • This variety is called biodiversity.
  • Plants and animals are often known by local names, which differ from place to place.
  • This creates confusion, so there is a need for a standard naming system understood worldwide.
  • The process of giving a standard name is called nomenclature, and it requires proper identification of the organism.
  • Plants follow the International Code for Botanical Nomenclature (ICBN).
  • Animals follow the International Code of Zoological Nomenclature (ICZN).
  • Scientific names are unique, accepted worldwide, and follow binomial nomenclature (two-part names).
  • Binomial nomenclature was introduced by Carolus Linnaeus.
  • Each name has a Generic name (genus) and a specific epithet (species).
  • Example: Mangifera indica (mango) — Mangifera is the genus and indica is the species.
  • Scientific names are in Latin and written in italics (or underlined when handwritten).
  • The genus starts with a capital letter.
  • The species starts with a small letter.
  • The author's abbreviated name is added (e.g., Mangifera indica Linn.).
  • Human beings have long been interested in knowing about different organisms, their diversity, and their relationships.
  • This study is called systematics, a word from Latin systema meaning systematic arrangement of organisms.
  • Linnaeus used the title Systema Naturae for his publication.
  • The scope of systematics later expanded to include identification, nomenclature, and classification.
  • Systematics also considers the evolutionary relationships between organisms.
  • Classification is a step-by-step process with a hierarchy of ranks called taxonomic categories, forming the taxonomic hierarchy.
  • Each category is a unit of classification called a taxon (plural: taxa).
  • Insects are a group with common features like three pairs of jointed legs, representing categories that denote a rank.
  • Taxonomic groups or categories are distinct biological entities, not just based on outward appearance.
  • Common taxonomical categories include: kingdom, phylum/division (plants), class, order, family, genus, and species.
  • Species is the lowest category for all organisms.
  • To place an organism in these categories, knowledge of its characters is needed.
  • This knowledge helps in finding similarities and differences among organisms of the same kind and with other kinds.

TAXONOMIC CATEGORIES

Species

Taxonomic studies say a species is a group of organisms that share basic similarities. We can tell one species apart from another by their clear morphological (physical) differences.

Examples:

  • Mangifera indica (mango)
  • Solanum tuberosum (potato)
  • Panthera leo (lion)

In these scientific names:

  • The second word (indica, tuberosum, leo) is the specific epithet (species name).
  • The first word (Mangifera, Solanum, Panthera) is the genus, a higher category.

A genus can have many species with similar features:

  • Panthera → includes leo (lion) and tigris (tiger)
  • Solanum → includes tuberosum (potato), nigrum, and melongena (brinjal)

Humans belong to the species sapiens, in the genus Homo. So, the scientific name of humans is Homo sapiens.

Genus

A genus is a group of related species that share more common features with each other than with species of other genera. In simple words, genera are collections of closely related species.

Examples:

  • Potato and brinjal are different species, but both belong to the genus Solanum.
  • Lion (Panthera leo), leopard (Panthera pardus), and tiger (Panthera tigris) share many features, so they all belong to the genus Panthera.
  • The genus Panthera is different from the genus Felis, which includes cats.

Family

A family is the next taxonomic category after genus. It groups together related genera (plural of genus). Families have fewer similarities compared to genus and species, but the organisms are still related.

Families are identified using both vegetative features (plant body) and reproductive features.

Examples:

  • In plants, the genera Solanum, Petunia, and Datura all belong to the family Solanaceae.
  • In animals, the genus Panthera (lion, tiger, leopard) and the genus Felis (cats) belong to the family Felidae.
  • Cats and dogs share some features but also differ, so they are placed in two different families: Felidae (cats) and Canidae (dogs).

Order

We saw earlier that species, genus, and family are grouped based on similar features. An order is a higher taxonomic category that groups together families which share some common characteristics.

These similarities are fewer compared to those found among genera within a family.

Examples:

  • In plants, the families Convolvulaceae and Solanaceae are placed in the order Polymoniales, mainly based on their floral features.
  • In animals, the order Carnivora includes families such as Felidae (cats, lions, tigers) and Canidae (dogs).

Class

A class is a taxonomic category that includes related orders. Orders grouped under a class share certain common characteristics.

Examples:

  • The order Primata (monkey, gorilla, gibbon) is placed in the class Mammalia.
  • The order Carnivora (tiger, cat, dog) is also part of the class Mammalia.
  • The class Mammalia includes many other orders in addition to Primata and Carnivora.

Phylum / Division

A phylum is the next higher taxonomic category after class. It groups together classes that share a set of fundamental characteristics. In plants, this category is called a division.

Examples:

  • In animals, classes such as fishes, amphibians, reptiles, birds, and mammals are grouped into the phylum Chordata because they possess a notochord and a dorsal hollow nerve cord.
  • In plants, classes that share some common features are grouped into a higher category called Division.

KINGDOM

  • All animals are grouped into the highest category called Kingdom Animalia.
  • All plants are grouped into a separate category called Kingdom Plantae.
  • Together, these are called the animal and plant kingdoms.
  • In classification, categories are arranged in ascending order from species to kingdom.
  • These categories are broad, so taxonomists created sub-categories to classify organisms more accurately.
  • As we move upward from species to kingdom, the number of common features decreases.
  • At lower levels (species, genus, etc.), organisms share more similarities.
  • At higher levels (kingdom), relationships between organisms become more difficult to identify, making classification more complex.

Summary

Diversity of the Living World

The living world shows a huge variety of organisms. Millions of plants and animals are known, but many are still undiscovered.

Need for Classification

Organisms differ in size, colour, habitat, body functions, and body structure. Therefore, clear characteristics are needed to define and study them properly.

Taxonomy

To study this diversity, biologists developed rules for identification, naming, and classification of organisms. This branch of science is called taxonomy.

Importance of Taxonomic Studies

Taxonomic studies are useful in agriculture, forestry, industry, and in understanding bio-resources and biological diversity.

International Rules and Scientific Naming

The basic principles of taxonomy—identification, naming, and classification—are followed worldwide under international codes. Each organism is given a scientific name of two words, known as binomial nomenclature.

Taxonomic Categories and Hierarchy

Every organism has a specific place in the classification system. There are many ranks called taxonomic categories or taxa, and together they form the taxonomic hierarchy.
